Cultural Resistance: From Imperial Russia to Post-Soviet States

Conference | Amherst Center for Russian Culture, Amherst College | March 28–29, 2026

A large-scale project combining a two-day in-person conference (March 2026) with a series of online workshops running from February through May 2026. The project examines cultural resistance as a field of inquiry across the history of the Russian Empire, the Soviet Union, and post-Soviet states — exploring its actors, methods, strategies, and sites, both real and virtual, historical and contemporary.

The online workshop series is designed to create a sustained intellectual community around the topic. Each session brings together a deliberately diverse group: established scholars from American and European universities, recent emigrants from Russia and other post-Soviet countries who are rebuilding their academic careers in the West, and graduate students at the beginning of their research paths. This mix is intentional — the workshops serve not only as a scholarly forum but as a space of professional solidarity and cross-generational exchange for a dispersed intellectual community shaped by the very dynamics of resistance the project studies.

The conference itself brings together participants from across literary studies, history, anthropology, sociology, political science, and education.

My role: Organizer and convener.

Melodrama and Melodramatic Imagination in Russia

Closed international workshop | In partnership with the Slavic-Eurasian Research Center, Hokkaido University | October 2024 – May 2025

An invitation-only scholarly workshop series exploring melodrama as a mode of cultural expression in Russian and post-Soviet contexts — from 19th-century prose to contemporary cinema and politics. The project is part of a broader international research initiative led by Hokkaido University (Prof. Daisuke Adachi) and Illinois Wesleyan University (Prof. Marina Balina), supported by the JSPS Grant-in-Aid for Scientific Research. The collaborative work contributed to the forthcoming volume Melodrama and Melodramatic Imagination in Russia: New Perspectives (University of Toronto Press).

My role: Co-organizer of the workshop. Contributed a chapter on melodramatic narratives in 1830s Russian prose.

Bannye chteniya (Банные чтения)

Annual conference of the New Literary Review journal | Moscow | 2000–2009

One of the most innovative and intellectually vibrant academic events in 2000s Moscow. Bannye chteniya ("Bathhouse Readings") was an annual interdisciplinary conference that brought together scholars from across the social sciences and humanities alongside artists, writers, and social activists. The format deliberately broke with traditional academic conventions — fostering open, informal exchange between disciplines and between the academic world and civil society. The conference became a key meeting point for Russia's progressive intellectual community during that decade.

My role: Organizer (as a member of the editorial team of New Literary Review, where I led the history department for ten years, 2000–2010).
