"Dangerous Texts: Political Dissent in Russian Culture" — Student Research Project

Amherst College | Fall semester 2025

A collective research project carried out by the students of my course "Dangerous Texts: Political Dissent in Russian Culture." Working with the Thomas P. Whitney Collection preserved in the Amherst Center for Russian Culture archive, students investigated the publication history of Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn's The Gulag Archipelago outside the Soviet Union — a work originally translated into English by Whitney himself. The project resulted in an audio podcast exploring the extraordinary story of how the manuscript was smuggled out of the USSR and translated for Western audiences.

"Law and Justice in Russian Literature" — Student Contributions to a Museum Exhibition

Amherst College | Spring semester 2025

Students of my course "Law and Justice in Russian Literature" contributed research and interpretive materials to the exhibition Pictures from the "Dead House": Leonid Lamm's Prison Dialogue with Fyodor Dostoevsky at the Amherst Center for Russian Culture (curated by Maria Timina, Curator of Russian and European Art at the Mead Art Museum). The exhibition features a nearly complete set of lithographs by Soviet nonconformist artist Leonid Lamm (1928–2017), inspired by Dostoevsky's fictionalized memoir of imprisonment. Students explored the intersection of literature, law, and the experience of incarceration — connecting 19th-century texts to 20th-century artistic responses to political repression.

Contributing students: Sarah Clarridge '28, Lukas Luby-Prikot '26, Buyanzul Olzvoi '28, Madina Oraz '25.

Minor "Soviet and Post-Soviet Culture"

National Research University — Higher School of Economics, Moscow | 2019–2024

Designed and directed a university-wide academic minor open to undergraduate students across all faculties of the Higher School of Economics. The program included courses on Soviet and post-Soviet culture, urban modernization in the Soviet Union, and Soviet culture and memory studies. The minor offered students an interdisciplinary framework for understanding the cultural and intellectual legacies of the Soviet period — combining approaches from cultural studies, history, philosophy, and visual analysis.

My role: Creator and academic director of the minor — developed the curriculum from scratch, designed individual courses, recruited and coordinated teaching faculty, oversaw the program's integration into the university structure.

Research Project: Sociology of Soviet Literature

Higher School of Economics, Moscow | September 2020 – February 2022

A collaborative research project examining the social structures, institutional networks, and professional dynamics of Soviet literary life. The project involved undergraduate and graduate students and was part of the larger university research initiative "Literature as Cultural Practice and Social Experience."

My role: Head of the project — designed the research framework, supervised student researchers, managed the project timeline and deliverables.

Research Projects: History of Soviet Education and the Cold War

Russian Presidential Academy of National Economy and Public Administration (RANEPA), Moscow | 2013–2018

A series of research projects examining the history of Soviet educational policy and its entanglement with Cold War cultural politics.

Resulting collective monograph: Islands of Utopia: Social and Pedagogical Approaches to Modeling Post-WWII School (Novoe literaturnoe obozrenie, 2015, co-edited with P. Safronov and I. Kukulin).

My role: Head of the project — led the research team, secured funding, designed the project, contributed original research.

Collective Research Projects at the New Literary Review (NLO)

Moscow | 2000–2010

As head of the history department at the New Literary Review, I led collective research projects that resulted in two interdisciplinary volumes bringing together scholars from cultural studies, history, anthropology, and literary criticism:

  • 1990: An Experience of Studying Recent History (first published as a special double issue of NLO, No. 83/84, 2007; republished as a two-volume book by Novoe literaturnoe obozrenie, 2011; co-edited with A. Dmitriev, I. Kukulin, O. Timofeeva, and A. Reitblat) — a landmark collective study of the last year of the Soviet empire, combining analytical articles, private diaries (from diplomats, artists, activists), day-by-day chronicles, and expert commentary. Described by the publisher as "an encyclopedia of late-Soviet civilization on the brink of collapse." An abridged English edition was published as 1990: Russians Remember a Turning Point (Quercus, 2013, translated by Arch Tait).
  • Merry Little Guys: Culture Heroes of Soviet Childhood (Novoe literaturnoe obozrenie, 2008, co-edited with M. Lipovetsky and I. Kukulin) — an exploration of iconic characters and cultural figures that shaped the imagination of Soviet children across generations.
  • Anthropology of Revolution (Novoe literaturnoe obozrenie, 2009, co-edited with I. Prokhorova, A. Dmitriev, and I. Kukulin) — a multidisciplinary examination of revolutionary experience and its cultural representations.

My role: Head of the research collective and co-editor — conceived the projects, coordinated contributors, and edited the volumes.

Karamzin Fellowships for Young Scholars

RANEPA, Moscow | Funded by the Mikhail Prokhorov Foundation | 2013–2018

Administered a competitive fellowship program providing scholarship funding for collective academic projects at the Centre of Humanitarian Studies at RANEPA. The Karamzin Scholarships, funded by the Mikhail Prokhorov Foundation (one of Russia's most prominent private cultural foundations), supported young researchers in the humanities and social sciences.

My role: Head of the search committee and program administrator — managed the selection process, evaluated applications, and oversaw the fellows' progress.
